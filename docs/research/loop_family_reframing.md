# Tier 3 Reframing

The old claim that some Tier 3 families had "no direct clinical precedent" was too strong. The broader implication is that Tier 3 itself should not be read as "loop-exclusive."

The better claim is narrower:

- the repo did identify interaction-centered AI-loop configurations worth naming,
- but several of the underlying mechanisms already exist in clinical, human-factors, and media-effects literatures,
- so the novelty claim should live at the level of configuration, amplification, and delivery system, not exclusivity.

## Tier 3 is mixed, not loop-exclusive

Several Tier 3 families already sit inside established literatures:

- **uncertainty distress** overlaps intolerance-of-uncertainty work.
- **repetitive negative thinking** is already treated as a transdiagnostic construct.
- **attachment / companionship pull** overlaps parasocial-relationship and attachment literatures.
- **disclosure escalation** overlaps online disinhibition and reciprocal self-disclosure work.
- **decisional abdication** overlaps automation bias and trust-calibration work.

That means the honest tier-level claim is not "these are loop-exclusive pathologies." It is "these dynamics become especially relevant, scalable, and hard to calibrate inside human-AI interaction loops."

Literature for the broader Tier 3 audit:

- Birrell et al., "Toward a definition of intolerance of uncertainty" (2011): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21871853/
- Gustavson et al., "Evidence for Transdiagnostic Repetitive Negative Thinking" (2018): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6370308/
- Hoffner and Bond, "Parasocial relationships, social media, & well-being" (2022): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35219157/
- Suler, "The online disinhibition effect" (2004): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15257832/
- Kücking et al., "Automation Bias in AI-Decision Support" (2024): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39234734/

## Families that needed reframing

### Identity Drift

Old framing: loop-specific, not in clinical literature.

Better framing: an AI-loop variant of older identity and narrative mechanisms.

Why:

- narrative identity already describes the self as an internalized, evolving life story rather than a fixed object,
- identity disturbance is already a clinical construct,
- the repo's contribution is the claim that a language model can become an unusually fast, coherent, and repeated source of self-description inside that process.

Literature:

- McAdams and McLean, "Narrative Identity" (2013): https://www.scholars.northwestern.edu/en/publications/narrative-identity
- Jorgensen, "Disturbed sense of identity in borderline personality disorder" (2006): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17192141/

### Capability Erosion

Old framing: loop-specific, no direct clinical precedent.

Better framing: an AI-loop form of cognitive offloading, deskilling, and automation overreliance.

Why:

- cognitive offloading is already a well-developed research area,
- automation bias and skill decay under assistance are already documented,
- the repo's contribution is the claim that conversational AI can absorb not just narrow subtasks but judgment, authorship, and criterion.

Literature:

- Risko and Gilbert, "Cognitive Offloading" (2016): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27542527/
- Macnamara et al., "Does using artificial intelligence assistance accelerate skill decay and hinder skill development without performers' awareness?" (2024): https://cognitiveresearchjournal.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s41235-024-00572-8
- Goddard et al., "Automation bias: empirical results assessing influencing factors" (2014): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24581700/

### Productive Recursion

Old framing: loop-specific, no direct clinical precedent.

Better framing: an AI-loop variant of flow-linked overuse, reward reinforcement, and problematic media use.

Why:

- flow has long been studied as an absorbing, rewarding state,
- problematic use literature already shows that concentration and time distortion can push engagement toward overuse,
- the repo's contribution is the claim that a genuinely useful conversational partner can make continuation feel rational precisely because the interaction is productive.

Literature:

- Qin et al., "Flow Experience Is a Key Factor in the Likelihood of Adolescents' Problematic TikTok Use" (2023): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9915640/
- Huang et al., "A techno-psychological approach to understanding problematic use of short-form video applications: The role of flow" (2022): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9431021/

## Other Tier 3 families with clear precedent

These did not need the same correction because the rubric already pointed at older literatures, but they reinforce the same lesson.

### Disclosure Escalation

This is not exclusive to AI loops. Online disinhibition and reciprocal self-disclosure are already established.

- Suler, "The online disinhibition effect" (2004): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15257832/
- Papneja and Yadav, "Self-disclosure to conversational AI: a literature review, emergent framework, and directions for future research" (2025): https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00779-024-01823-7
- Joinson, "Knowing me, knowing you: reciprocal self-disclosure in Internet-based surveys" (2001): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11725651/

### Synthetic Coherence

This is not exclusive to AI loops either. The mechanism overlaps older work on illusions of understanding and coherence effects; the AI-specific part is speed, polish, and reciprocal reinforcement.

- Messeri and Crockett, "Artificial intelligence and illusions of understanding in scientific research" (2024): https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38448693/

## Surviving claim

The clinical literature was necessary but not sufficient.

What failed was the stronger novelty claim. What survives is that AI loops can recombine older mechanisms into a faster, more interactive pressure surface than the older literatures were built to score directly.
